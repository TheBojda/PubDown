# Afterword

The circle has closed.

If we accept that the most optimal form of any intelligent life is
digital existence---an evolutionary direction toward which such life
inevitably strives---then humanity itself seems destined to ultimately
dwell in simulated worlds.

Only one question remains: is the reality we currently experience the
primary, objective reality... or just a simulation?

And even if our world is not an artificially engineered construct, there
is still a real possibility that it is embedded within a deeper, more
fundamental reality. As we explored in the chapter on brain universes,
our universe may be a kind of emergent simulation---not generated by a
computer, but by a thinking universe. Perhaps we all live inside "the
dream of God."

Although I believe this book stands as a complete whole, I am well aware
that countless topics had to be left untouched. For instance, the moral
and ethical implications of the simulation hypothesis, or the many ways
in which such technology could go terribly wrong. History has taught us
that humanity possesses a peculiar talent for weaponizing its greatest
inventions. I chose not to dive into these dystopian paths because many
others have already done so---just think of the majority of Black Mirror
episodes.

Despite that, I remain optimistic. Partly because if the kind of
simulation I discussed in the first part is indeed possible, then
immersion in such a simulation would actually mean immersion in our own
minds---or perhaps in a shared space composed of interconnected minds.
Such a world would be a very different kind of simulation than those
dark visions warn us about: a private, internal experience rather than a
virtual marketplace driven by the same capitalist rules as today's
society. Not like the world of Upload, where even simulated experiences
come with a price tag.

Moreover, I believe that the "godlike human" of the future---the Homo
Deus---will eventually leave behind not only the biological body, but
also the resource-scarcity mindset and value systems that our
civilization currently clings to. Concepts like capitalism, or even
money itself, may become entirely meaningless. So might democracy, which
at its core is institutionalized compromise by the minority. In a
post-singularity world, anyone who disagrees with the majority could
simply create their own universe and populate it with those who think
alike.

A reality beyond the singularity would be radically different from
ours---and there's every reason to believe it could be better, not
worse.

If this topic has sparked your curiosity, I encourage you to keep
exploring. There are countless films, books, articles, and videos on the
subject. I hope this book has provided enough intellectual ammunition to
help you continue your journey, should you wish to dig deeper.

And finally, let's not forget the true purpose of this book: thinking
about simulated reality is worth it because it is fun.

I sincerely hope, dear reader, that you enjoyed reading this book at
least as much as I enjoyed writing it. If you did, then it was all worth
it.
