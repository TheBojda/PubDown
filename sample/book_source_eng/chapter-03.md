# How Einstein’s Theory of Relativity Redefined Space and Time

I was around twelve years old when I first encountered Einstein's
Special Theory of Relativity. It was covered in the final chapter of a
hefty book titled Modern Physics. I don't remember how I stumbled upon
it, but I was instantly captivated---so much so that for a few years, I
seriously considered becoming a physicist. As I read that chapter, I
felt like Alice suddenly transported to Wonderland---except in this
case, Wonderland wasn't some magical, far-off land, but the very world I
had always lived in...

When Albert Einstein unveiled his theory of Special Relativity, he did
more than propose a new scientific framework --- he upended the
foundational assumptions that had guided physics for centuries. Up to
that point, Newtonian mechanics had dominated, portraying space and time
as static, immutable entities. These dimensions were seen not as
participants in the workings of the universe, but as a neutral backdrop
for its events.

Einstein, with his unorthodox thinking, shattered that view. He
introduced a radical notion: everything (including time) is relative,
dependent on the observer's frame of reference.

This revolutionary concept extended far beyond equations. It hinted that
even our most basic understandings of time and space could be fluid and
subject to reinterpretation. That spirit of questioning convention may
be what truly elevates Einstein among the greatest scientific minds in
history.

Little wonder, then, that if you ask a random person to name a
scientist, "Einstein" is almost always the first name that comes to
mind.

To understand Special Relativity, we need to travel back to the 1800s.
At that time, science had two incredibly successful models describing
the world. The first was Newtonian mechanics, which explained how
objects move. The second was Maxwell's set of equations, which described
electromagnetic forces, including how light behaves. The problem was
that these two theories didn't play well together. Rather than getting
too scientific, let's explore this mismatch through a simple thought
experiment.

Picture two spaceships in motion. One is flying away from Earth at 5,000
km/h, and the other is heading toward Earth at that same speed. To
someone riding on the ship moving away, the incoming ship would appear
to be closing in at 10,000 km/h. That's perfectly reasonable. Speed is
always measured to something --- be it Earth, the Sun, or another
object. So in this case, the relative speed between the ships is 10,000
km/h.

Now, imagine a person throws an object from one spaceship toward the
other at 100 km/h. The receiving ship would encounter it at a relative
velocity of 10,100 km/h. So far, everything fits with our everyday
intuition.

But things take a strange turn when we replace the thrown object with a
beam of light. According to both experimental data and theoretical
models, light doesn't arrive at lightspeed + 10,000 km/h. Instead, it
always arrives at the same constant speed: the speed of light. It
doesn't care how fast the ships are moving or in what direction. Light
refuses to adjust its speed for anyone.

This unusual behavior stemmed from a profound contradiction between
Newtonian physics and the theory of electromagnetism, which was
experimentally confirmed by the landmark Michelson--Morley experiment.

Since light acts like a wave, similar to ripples on water, scientists
assumed it must travel through some medium. They proposed the existence
of an invisible substance filling all of space, called the "luminiferous
ether." If light waves moved through this ether, Earth's motion should
influence how fast light appears to move, depending on direction.

In simplified terms, the experiment split a beam of light and sent the
parts in perpendicular directions --- one aligned with Earth's motion
and one at a right angle to it. Upon recombination, if the speeds
differed, when they interfere with each other, it would reveal the
difference. But there was no difference between the speed of light
beams, which meant that the Earth's motion could not be detected
relative to the ether. The speed of light remained unchanged, regardless
of Earth's movement.

Einstein's radical move was to stop trying to explain away this odd
result and instead accept it as an axiom. What if light's speed is
always the same for all observers, no matter how fast they're moving?
Let's take that as a starting principle, he proposed, and tweak Newton's
laws instead. But doing so meant rethinking how time works: it would
have to slow down for fast-moving objects.

We know that speed is defined by how much distance an object covers in a
given amount of time. We also know that if we move toward something at a
certain speed, we must add our own speed to the speed of that object,
and this gives us its relative speed as measured from our perspective
--- something that should also be true for light. The experiments did
not show this. But if the clock aboard the moving ship ticks more
slowly, then this change cancels out the motion effect, keeping light
speed constant. Mathematical calculations show exactly how much time
must "dilate" to make everything consistent.

But that's not the only thing that has to change. To preserve other
physical principles, like the conservation of energy and momentum,
length and mass must also be adjusted. Objects in motion must contract
slightly, and their mass must increase. Once these changes are applied,
all the laws of physics still work, now fully compatible with both
Newton's and Maxwell's laws.

Einstein's true genius was in flipping the equation. Rather than seeing
time, space, and mass as fundamental, he made the speed of light the
constant and let the rest bend to fit. This shift allowed both earlier
theories to coexist within a more comprehensive framework.

What's truly astonishing is that Einstein's radical idea --- essentially
nothing more than a clever mathematical adjustment ---is working, and it
turned out to describe the real world with remarkable precision.
Although we're not chasing light in spaceships, experiments involving
subatomic particles have repeatedly confirmed these effects. Fast-moving
particles decay more slowly than normal and gain mass just as Einstein
predicted. These adjustments are part of the daily routine in modern
particle physics labs.

It is less well known that even before Einstein's solution, a theory
already existed that explained the constancy of the speed of light.

Before Einstein's theory came onto the scene, physicists Hendrik Lorentz
and George Francis FitzGerald had already proposed a solution to the
puzzle of why motion through the supposed "ether" couldn't be detected.
Their approach didn't start with the idea that the speed of light was
always constant. Instead, it rested on the belief that light traveled
through a universal medium, the "luminiferous ether", which at the time
was the official scientific standpoint.

You might remember the classical atomic model from school: electrons
orbiting a central nucleus, held in place by electromagnetic forces.
Back then, these forces were thought to rely on the ether for
transmission. Lorentz hypothesized that if atoms moved through this
medium, their internal structure would distort slightly in the direction
of motion. This subtle squeezing effect would cause all matter --- being
made of atoms --- to contract. The math behind this shrinkage became
known as the Lorentz contraction, which is identical to what Einstein
later predicted using a completely different line of reasoning.

Although Lorentz's model was eventually abandoned, it was not because it
made incorrect predictions. Rather, it was superseded by Einstein's
formulation, which was more streamlined, required fewer assumptions, and
applied more generally. Still, Lorentz's contribution is not forgotten:
the transformation equations (Lorentz transformation) used in special
relativity still bear his name.

For most people, the concept of space remains rooted in Newton's idea of
an absolute, unchanging arena --- something that exists independently of
what's in it. If two objects are one meter apart, they are assumed to be
one meter apart for everyone, in all frames of reference. That feels
like common sense. But modern physics tells us this view doesn't reflect
reality.

Back when the definition of a meter was based on a platinum rod stored
in Paris, distances were thought to be rigid and universal. But Lorentz
raised an intriguing possibility: what if that meter stick itself
shrinks as it moves? And what if every measuring tool contracts by the
same proportion? In that case, we'd still measure the same distances,
but what we perceive as "one meter" would actually be shorter. Rather
than saying that every measuring rod has contracted, it's more accurate
--- and less confusing --- to say that space itself contracted.

> *This leads us to a deeper realization: the classical notion of
> absolute space is no longer physically meaningful. Physics deals with
> what can be measured. And if every ruler in motion gets shorter, then
> so does the space itself, which is contracted.*

If the speed of light were significantly slower, and relativistic
effects were more pronounced in everyday life, we might not have
developed the concept of space as something absolute. A car zipping past
us might visibly flatten in its direction of motion, and we'd think of
that as completely normal. In that alternate reality, the idea of
absolute, immutable space might never have entered human thought at all.

So, it makes sense that Newton's absolute space eventually gave way to
something more practical: measurable, relative space. But when it comes
to time, the confusion cuts even deeper. How is it possible that an
astronaut traveling near the speed of light can age more slowly than a
twin who stays on Earth?

Here, too, we must distinguish between two concepts: absolute time, as
imagined by Newton, and physical time --- the kind we measure with
clocks. And "clocks" don't just mean ticking mechanisms. Any regular
process --- such as radioactive decay or human aging --- serves as a
timekeeper. Aging is a complex chemical process, and these processes,
rooted in physical interactions, especially electromagnetic ones, can be
affected by motion. If motion distorts those interactions, then the
entire process, including aging, slows down.

> *While we could theoretically preserve the idea of absolute time, it
> becomes meaningless in practical terms. Everything we know about time
> comes from physical processes, and all of those are subject to the
> effects of motion. If absolute time can't be observed or measured,
> then in the language of physics, it doesn't really exist.*

The concept of mass faces a similar rethinking. We're used to thinking
of mass as the "amount of stuff" in an object. But in physics, mass is
more accurately defined by how much an object resists being pushed ---
its inertia. Push a heavy object and a light one with the same force,
and the lighter one accelerates more. That's how Newton defines the mass
of an object: by how much force is needed to accelerate it.

We exert force through physical interactions --- specifically, four
fundamental forces: gravity, electromagnetism, and the strong and weak
nuclear forces. Of these, gravity in Einstein's general theory is no
longer a "force" in the traditional sense but a warping of space itself.
The strong force is confined to atomic nuclei. The remaining two ---
electromagnetic and weak --- were unified in the 1970s under the term
"electroweak interaction." The electromagnetic force is what we
typically use to apply forces to objects.

Imagine a particle accelerator where an electromagnetic field
accelerates particles. As a particle approaches very high speeds, it
requires much more energy to accelerate further. This can be interpreted
in two ways: either that the particle's mass has increased, or that, due
to its rapid motion, the electromagnetic field---transmitted through the
ether---acts on the particle less effectively.

My aim in presenting the Lorentz interpretation was not to argue against
Einstein's version, but to highlight a perspective that personally
helped me a lot in understanding Special Relativity. One of the
challenges in understanding Einstein's theory is that it uses the same
words as Newton---space and time---yet means something completely
different by them. When we think about time and length, we instinctively
picture Newton's abstract, absolute framework, whereas Einstein's time
and length are measured quantities, tied directly to clocks and rulers.
The distinction seems subtle, but it is absolutely fundamental.

Lorentz's interpretation clearly reveals the gap between the concepts
formed in our minds and physical reality itself. The idea of absolute
time arises naturally from our everyday experience, and we take it as
something fundamental --- but it only works because, at ordinary speeds,
physical interactions and chemical reactions occur at fixed rates. At
very high speeds, these reactions slow down significantly, and as a
result, every time-measuring process --- including our own aging ---
also slows down. If this effect were noticeable at everyday speeds, we
would probably never have developed the very notion of absolute time.

Lorentz assumed a deeper underlying explanation for the observed
phenomena --- an interaction with the ether --- while Einstein derived
Special Relativity from the constancy of the speed of light and was
content to accept that this is how the world works. Since Einstein's
theory was simpler, more elegant, and capable of predicting more
phenomena, it ultimately prevailed. At the same time, it rendered the
concept of the ether unnecessary.

That raises a question: if relativity removed the ether, what happened
to it? Was it simply discarded? In a way, yes --- but not entirely. Like
absolute space and time, the ether faded from physics because it
couldn't be detected. If something has no observable effect, then
physics sets it aside. But then, what carries a light wave through empty
space?

Einstein's answer was simple: nothing needs to carry it. Light travels
through space itself. Space is no longer just an empty void --- it
behaves like a medium. In a sense, Einstein redefined the ether instead
of abolishing it. Space itself became the medium, capable of supporting
electromagnetic waves and responding to the presence of mass and energy.
Under general relativity, this flexible space is what curves to create
gravity.

So, rather than vanishing, the ether transformed. It became space, not a
passive, inert background, but an active, dynamic entity.

Perhaps the most important lesson we can learn from the theory of
relativity is that only observable things have physical reality. This
principle will become even more prominent in the next chapter, which
deals with quantum mechanics, but it is also fundamental to
understanding relativity itself.

Abstract concepts like Newtonian absolute space and time have no
physical meaning---in fact, they can mislead us. Sometimes, progress
requires us to abandon such concepts that lack physical significance and
replace them with more useful ones. Physical time is what clocks
measure; it is not a fundamental quantity, but rather an emergent
property of how matter behaves.

When we say that "time passes more slowly inside fast-moving spacecraft
or near massive celestial bodies," it sounds strange and mysterious. But
if we instead say that "within fast-moving spacecraft or near massive
celestial bodies, the interactions between particles change in such a
way that any system capable of measuring time---whether it's a
mechanical clock, an atomic clock, or human aging---will measure a
slower passage of time," the phenomenon becomes much more comprehensible
and less mysterious. Yet both statements are entirely equivalent.

This relativity of space and time fundamentally shakes our belief in an
absolute reality and highlights the fact that our picture of the
world---our mental model---is only an approximation of the truth. At the
same time, the theory of relativity remains intuitively graspable. In my
case, understanding Lorentz's alternative interpretation helped a great
deal in developing this intuition.

Despite its revolutionary implications, Relativity is still a classical
theory. It doesn't challenge the existence of an objective reality;
rather, it emphasizes how different this objective reality is from what
we might intuitively expect. In the next chapter, I will introduce the
other great theory that describes our universe: quantum mechanics.
Unlike relativity, quantum mechanics didn't sit well even with Einstein
himself, as it not only questions our fundamental concepts but also
challenges the very existence of an objective reality.
