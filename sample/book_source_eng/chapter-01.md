# Foreword

The idea that the reality we experience is nothing more than an illusion
is almost as old as humanity itself. Many religions touch on this
concept. One of the earliest non-religious mentions can be traced back
to the philosopher Plato and his famous Allegory of the Cave.

In Plato's thought experiment, prisoners are chained inside a cave from
birth. Their only connection to the outside world is the shadows cast on
the opposite wall by a light streaming through an opening. For them,
these shadows are the world. Should one of them escape, the true world
beyond the cave would feel alien and incomprehensible.

Although Plato intended the allegory to explain his theory of Forms and
the nature of knowledge, it is difficult not to see clear parallels with
modern interpretations. Consider the universe of The Matrix, where
humans live their entire lives restrained, immersed in a fabricated
world projected into their minds by intelligent machines. It is
essentially a 2,000-year-old idea retold with modern technology. The
simulation hypothesis is a rethinking of Plato's cave.

I must have been six or eight years old when this question first formed
in my mind: What if what I'm experiencing isn't real? What if my life is
nothing more than a reality show, like The Truman Show, or I am simply a
brain in a vat, kept by aliens like a strange houseplant or exotic
pet---entertaining them as they watch my life unfold on some enormous
television screen?

And I don't think I'm alone in this. Nearly everyone, at some point in
their life, wonders: Is this truly reality? Could this all be just a
dream---maybe a bad one, or perhaps a very good one?

But for me, the thought never let go. I have always been curious about
how reality actually works. As a teenager, I promised myself that one
day I would write a book on the subject. And now, about 30 years later,
that promise is finally taking shape.

The timing is no accident. Those decades were not only necessary for the
ideas in my mind to mature into something worthy of a book---they were
also needed for our technology to catch up. Today, companies are
investing millions of dollars in connecting brains to machines. The most
famous of these is Elon Musk's Neuralink. Advances in brain--computer
interfaces could make fully immersive virtual reality possible. The real
question is not if, but when.

This book is divided into three main parts:

Part One argues in favor of the Simulation Hypothesis: the proposal that
our world might already be a kind of illusion. To explore this, we will
look into modern physics---particularly quantum mechanics---and examine
some of its strangest interpretations. Once we explore the alternatives,
we'll realize that the simulation hypothesis isn't even the most bizarre
among them.

Part Two asks how we might create our own simulation. I will argue that
building such a simulation could be the best thing that can ever happen
to humanity---and perhaps the only viable path toward a truly
sustainable future.

Part Three looks toward our possible future. Here, we will explore
transhumanism: how humans might achieve immortality, and how our species
could evolve from Homo sapiens into what Yuval Noah Harari calls Homo
deus---a "god-like human." In this vision, we may one day leave our
biological bodies behind and live inside digital universes of our own
creation.

Each part begins with a short science fiction story---designed to spark
curiosity and prime the imagination for what follows. I hope my readers
will find them enjoyable.

The first story was born on Christmas Day, 2024. On a whim, I decided to
send it to Andy Weir. Most people know him as the author behind *The
Martian* or *Project Hail Mary*, but I was captivated by an earlier work
of his---a mini-story called *The Egg*. It was incredibly inspiring to
me, and I thought it was worth a try to send him my own, much more
sci-fi take on a similar theme. I was sure I wouldn't get a response.

To my surprise, Andy Weir *did* reply:

> *"You know what? Usually, I don't read things sent to me by people. I
> just don't have the time. But I thought I'd give this one a try and
> I'm glad I did. It's a very sweet story. Good work."*

His response was short, but it gave me a tremendous boost, for which I
would like to thank him. In part, it was thanks to this encouragement
that the book you're now holding finally got written.

And now, let's begin with *A Christmas Story*...
